Project 1: Rasterizer

The goal of this project was to develop a simple vector graphics renderer demonstrating many of the concepts introduced in the first leg of this course, including barycentric coordinates, anti-aliasing, hierarchical texture mapping, and perspective transforms to display SVG files.

Task 1: Drawing Single-Color Triangles

While rasterizing points and lines is a straightforward task, rasterizing triangles presents a unique challenge. How do we determine the internal points of a triangle given its vertices? Our solution is to sample every pixel in the frame buffer that is within the bounding box of the triangle, or the minimum orthogonal rectangle that contains the triangle’s vertices.

For each sampled point, we test if the triangle contains it by performing a separate test on each of the triangle’s edges. The point XXX﻿ is considered in the interior if, for edge AB‾\overline{AB}AB﻿, ABX‾\overline{ABX}ABX﻿ turns in the same direction as the triangle. That is, if the triangle’s vertices are defined in clockwise order, then ABXABXABX﻿ must be a clockwise turn from the starting point AAA﻿.

Satisfying this condition for all three edges is necessary and sufficient for a point to be considered in the triangle’s interior. If so, then it is assigned the given color in the frame buffer.

Because each sample is checked in constant time, the algorithm is no slower than one which samples every pixel in the bounding box.

Task 2: Antialiasing by Supersampling

While the rasterized triangles are geometrically accurate, the graphical fidelity is low; notice the jagged edges and visual artifacts on the two skinny triangles! It turns out our sampling method has a major flaw; since a sampled pixel is either in the triangle or not, and thus is either colored or not, at low resolutions edges will “step” at discreet points and produce a jarring visual effect. The antialiasing approach we used was supersampling: taking advantage of the fact that triangles exist in a continuous coordinate space, we can subdivide each pixel into s×ss \times ss×s﻿ supersamples, and draw in each pixel the average of its supersamples.

The only modification to the triangle rasterization algorithm was to perform this subdivision over the bounding box of the triangle; that is, instead of iterating one pixel at a time, we increment by 1/s1 / s1/s﻿ pixels.

Observe the result of antialiasing on the aforementioned skinny triangles: at higher sampling rates, the edges of the triangle look smooth, almost to the point of looking like continuous lines. To see why this is the case, recall the lack of granularity in the base case: instead of the binary of a point being shaded or not, supersampling shades points to varying degrees depending on their level of closeness to the triangle boundary. What originally appears as jagged edges is smoothed once boundary points which are close but not fully inside the triangle are taken into account.

While antialiasing by supersampling improves visual accuracy noticeably, it does so at the cost of a quadratic increase in running time and memory relative to the scale factor of the sample buffer. This is because the size of the sample buffer increases, and we still must iterate over it in its entirety.

Task 5: “Pixel Sampling” with Texture Mapping

The previous results have demonstrated how to rasterize triangles to display rudimentary color graphics. However, sometimes we will need to rasterize something with more detail, like a photographic image. For this, we use textured triangles, mapping from positions (x,y)(x, y)(x,y)﻿ in the frame buffer space to positions (u,v)(u, v)(u,v)﻿ in a texture space. This is implemented in a similar fashion to barycentric coloring; only the triangle vertices are mapped to texture points, and the interior is interpolated.

An intuitive way of conceptualizing this is as if a triangle was cut out of the texture image and then stretched and warped until it fits into the corresponding triangle in the raster image.

The method by which texture points are interpolated can be done in several different ways. One is to perform “nearest-neighbor” sampling; since an interpolated texture point (u,v)(u, v)(u,v)﻿ won’t fit perfectly within the pixel grid of the texture image, we can simply round it to the nearest lattice point.

However, this lacks granularity. What if the interpolated (u,v)(u, v)(u,v)﻿ sits in the middle of two texture pixels? We can use information from both by performing a linear interpolation based on the interpolated point’s distance.

It’s apparent that bilinear interpolation imparts sort of an antialiasing effect. This is understandable given that it acts in a similar manner; sampling information from multiple points when rendering a single pixel.

Task 6: “Level Sampling” with Mipmaps for Texture Mapping

Level sampling provides a solution to aliasing that is more preventative. The intuition is that the more a texture has to be magnified, the more aliasing will occur, and we can instead sample the texture at a lower resolution. To do this, we compute an image pyramid of increasingly subsampled texture maps, known as mipmaps, and determine the mipmap at which we want to sample a raster point based on its gradient: how much moving from that raster point will affect the texture point. The larger the gradient, the higher the mipmap level we want, and the lower the resolution of the texture.

At this point, we have achieved a combination of different pixel-based and level-based sampling techniques — in all, over 4×2×3=244 \times 2 \times 3 = 244×2×3=24﻿ viewing configurations are possible! Each optimization carries its own costs and benefits: supersampling improves granularity at the cost of memory and speed, and bilinear pixel sampling improves accuracy over a constant-factor slowdown (since you have to sample four texture points instead of one). Nearest-level sampling is comparable in performance to level-zero sampling (the only difference is a constant-time level calculation), whereas bilinear level interpolation slows down performance by a factor of 2 (since you sample two levels instead of one). Note that pixel and level sampling do not result in non-nominal changes to memory usage.
